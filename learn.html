Introduction to Geometry

Geometry is the branch of mathematics that studies shapes, sizes, and the properties of space. From the floor beneath your feet to the screen you're reading now — geometry is everywhere.

What is Geometry?

The word geometry comes from the Greek words geo (earth) and metron (measurement). Ancient Egyptians used it to resurvey land after Nile floods; Greek mathematicians like Euclid turned it into a formal science.

In this course you will explore 2D shapes — flat figures with length and width — and finish with an introduction to 3D shapes that also have depth.

2D Shapes

Rectangle, Circle, Triangle, Square, Parallelogram, Trapezium

Key measures

Area, Perimeter, Circumference, Volume

Real uses

Architecture, Art, Engineering, Navigation, Sports

💡 Did You Know?

Euclid's Elements, written around 300 BC, is one of the most influential textbooks in history — it was used in schools for over 2,000 years!

  • Geometry helps architects design safe buildings
  • GPS navigation relies on geometric calculations
  • Video game graphics are built entirely from geometric shapes
  • Medical imaging (MRI / CT) uses geometry to reconstruct 3D body images

Rectangle

A rectangle is a four-sided shape with four right angles. Opposite sides are equal and parallel.

Area = Length × Breadth  |  Perimeter = 2 × (l + b)

Key Properties

  • 4 right angles (each = 90°)
  • Opposite sides are equal in length
  • Diagonals bisect each other and are equal
  • A square is a special rectangle where all sides are equal

Real-world Applications

Rectangles are the most common shape in construction. Rooms, doors, windows, screens, and swimming pools are almost always rectangular.

Example

Room: l = 5 m, b = 4 m
Area = 20 m²

Example

Football pitch: 105 × 68 m
Area = 7,140 m²

Circle

A circle is a perfectly round shape where every point on its boundary is the same distance from the centre, called the radius.

Area = π r²  |  Circumference = 2π r  |  π ≈ 3.14159

Key Properties

  • Radius (r) — distance from centre to any boundary point
  • Diameter (d = 2r) — straight line through the centre
  • Circumference — the perimeter of a circle
  • π (pi) is a constant ≈ 3.14159, it never ends or repeats

💡 Fun Fact

Tyres, coins, wheels, and planets are all circular because the circle is the most efficient shape — it encloses the maximum area for a given perimeter.

Triangle

A triangle is a polygon with three sides and three angles. The sum of interior angles always equals 180°.

Area = ½ × base × height  |  Perimeter = a + b + c

Types of Triangle

Equilateral

All 3 sides equal · All angles = 60°

Isosceles

2 sides equal · 2 base angles equal

Scalene

All sides different · All angles different

Right-angled

One angle = 90° · Pythagoras applies: a²+b²=c²

💡 Fun Fact

Triangles are the strongest shape in engineering — that's why bridges, roof trusses, and the Eiffel Tower are full of triangles.

Square & Parallelogram

Two closely related four-sided shapes with distinct properties and formulas.

Square

A square is a rectangle where all four sides are equal. All angles are 90°.

Area = side²  |  Perimeter = 4 × side

Parallelogram

A parallelogram has two pairs of parallel sides. Opposite sides are equal; opposite angles are equal. Unlike a rectangle, angles are not necessarily 90°.

Area = base × height  |  Perimeter = 2 × (a + b)

Trapezium

A trapezium (called a trapezoid in the US) has exactly one pair of parallel sides, called the bases.

Area = ½ × (a + b) × h

Properties

  • Exactly one pair of parallel sides (the two bases)
  • The two non-parallel sides are called legs
  • An isosceles trapezium has equal legs and equal base angles
  • A right trapezium has two right angles

Real-world Uses

Trapezium shapes appear in dam cross-sections, handbags, road signs, and architectural facades.

3D Shapes

Three-dimensional shapes have length, width, and depth. They occupy space, which we measure as volume.

Common 3D Shapes

Cube

Volume = s³
Surface Area = 6s²
e.g. dice, Rubik's cube

Cuboid

Volume = l × w × h
Surface Area = 2(lw + lh + wh)
e.g. boxes, rooms

Sphere

Volume = 4/3 πr³
Surface Area = 4πr²
e.g. ball, planet

Cylinder

Volume = πr²h
Surface Area = 2πr(r+h)
e.g. can, pipe

Cone

Volume = 1/3 πr²h
e.g. ice-cream cone, traffic cone

Pyramid

Volume = 1/3 × base area × h
e.g. Great Pyramid of Giza

💡 Amazing Fact
